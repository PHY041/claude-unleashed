# Chapter 6: Shipping Products With an AI Team ## The Five-Hour Rebuild *What it looks like to build software when Claude Code is your engineering team — the workflows, the patterns, and the things that still go wrong.* --- ## Opening: Matt's Five Hours Matt Shields spent eight months watching a development agency build something he wasn't entirely sure he understood. That's not quite right — he understood what it did. He understood the user flows, the main screens, the approval logic. He'd attended every sprint review. He knew the product intimately. What he didn't understand was the code. And for about a year, he didn't need to. The application worked. His first three paying customers were happy with it. He was busy selling and supporting and learning the market. The codebase was someone else's problem. The problem arrived on an unremarkable Tuesday in October 2025 when a customer asked for a feature Matt had promised in the sales process: a notification when a document was superseded by a newer revision. Should be straightforward. Matt opened a project on Upwork, described the feature, and a freelance developer took the job. Three days later, the developer sent an estimate. Four to six hours of work, maybe more. He needed time to understand how the notification system was wired into the document versioning model, which was itself entangled in three abstraction layers the original agency had built. Matt read the estimate three times. "Four to six hours for a notification," he later described. "I knew something was wrong." He paid for a one-hour consultation with a second developer, a former senior engineer at a SaaS company, and asked her to look at the codebase and tell him what she saw. She spent the hour reading code. At the end she used a word that lodged in Matt's mind: impressive. The code was impressive but difficult. The original agency had built a system architected for theoretical extensibility — a service layer that abstracted the domain, an event bus that decoupled the domain from side effects, and a domain model deliberately isolated from both. Each layer was independently well-designed. The problem was that to do anything practical — add a feature, fix a behavior, understand what was happening — you needed to understand all three layers simultaneously, because every operation threaded through all three. There was no documentation explaining why the layers existed. The agency developers had moved to other clients. The reasoning lived in their heads, not in the codebase. Over the next four months, Matt hired two more developers to maintain the system. The first lasted three weeks before describing the codebase as "a beautiful mess I don't want to spend time in." He wasn't being uncharitable — this is what over-engineered codebases feel like from the inside. Every question requires reading three files instead of one. Every change requires understanding dependencies that aren't visible in the file you're editing. The cognitive tax is real and it accumulates. The second developer was more experienced and lasted six weeks. She made some genuine improvements — added documentation, simplified a few of the more tangled dependencies, wrote tests for the most critical paths. But she concluded what Matt was beginning to suspect: the cost of working around the existing architecture exceeded the value of any features he could add to it. --- In February 2026, Matt drew a line on a piece of paper. On one side: live with what he had, grow slowly, accept that every feature would cost $2,000-5,000 and two to four weeks because the codebase made everything hard. On the other side: start over. He'd been using Claude Code since November 2025, initially for small things. Scripts to process customer data. Analysis of his Stripe metrics. Drafts of customer communications. He'd graduated to having it write simple utility functions, then route handlers, then — cautiously, experimentally — small features. In January he'd watched a developer friend rebuild a small API service over a weekend. In real time. With Claude Code doing most of the implementation work. The friend was a competent developer who could have built the service manually; Claude Code just made it go dramatically faster. Matt filed this away. In March, he was on a call with a potential enterprise customer who wanted custom reporting capabilities that Matt's current system couldn't provide without significant work. He ended the call, sat for a moment, and thought: what if I just rebuilt it? Not the whole application — the critical path. The core features that 80 percent of users used 90 percent of the time. Project creation. Timeline management. Contractor invitation and coordination. Document upload and versioning. The approval workflow. Everything else could come after. He opened a terminal. It was Thursday at 1:00 PM. He had no idea it would be done before dinner. --- Before we get to the 1:00 PM start, it's worth understanding the emotional texture of what preceded it. Matt had been carrying the weight of this decision for four months. The bad codebase wasn't just a technical problem — it was a business risk. Every month that the codebase stayed difficult was a month where his competitors could move faster than he could. Every customer request that took three weeks instead of three days was a relationship at risk. Every time he had to tell a customer "that'll take us a few weeks to build" while knowing internally that the main difficulty was the architecture, he felt the compounding cost of that original $50,000 decision. He also felt the particular frustration of someone who's been burned. He'd done everything right the first time. He'd hired a reputable agency. He'd attended every sprint review. He'd written detailed specifications. He'd done the due diligence. He'd still ended up with something unmaintainable. The failure wasn't his fault, exactly, but it was his problem. The decision to rebuild wasn't impulsive. It was the conclusion of a months-long calculation that kept coming out the same way: the cost of continuing to maintain the old system exceeded the cost of replacing it. He'd run the numbers multiple ways. They always agreed. What made March the right month was the combination of two things. The enterprise customer call, which made clear that custom reporting was going to be table stakes for the tier of customer he was targeting. And a conversation with a developer friend who had just used Claude Code to rebuild a backend service in a weekend, in real time, in front of Matt. Not a toy service. A real one that was running in production. "That could be the whole thing," Matt remembered thinking during the call. Not "that could help me" — "that could be the whole thing." --- The first thirty minutes produced no code. Matt had learned from his earlier Claude Code use that the quality of the output was directly proportional to the quality of the context he provided. Good output required good setup. He decided to treat the setup as seriously as the implementation. He created a directory and a file called `CLAUDE.md`. If you haven't read Chapter 2, this is Claude Code's memory file — the document that tells Claude Code who you are, what you're building, what constraints matter, and what decisions have already been made. Every time Claude Code starts working on your project, it reads this file first. Here's what Matt wrote: ```markdown # ConstructSync Rebuild — Project Context ## What This Is B2B SaaS for construction project management. Core users: General contractors (GCs): - Create and manage projects - Invite subcontractors to specific work packages - Upload and version contract documents - Track approval status and who's holding things up Subcontractors (subs): - View assigned work packages - Download current document revisions - Submit deliverables for approval - See the status of their submissions ## Why We're Rebuilding Previous codebase: service layer + event bus + domain model, 340 files, 28K lines. Primary problem: too much implicit coupling. Adding a feature requires understanding 12+ files simultaneously because of abstraction layers nobody documented. New codebase must be: - Direct: database operations in server actions (no service layer middleman) - Readable: a developer who's never seen this code understands any file in 10 minutes - Minimal: abstract only when the abstraction removes duplication without hiding meaning ## Tech Stack - Next.js 14 with App Router (server components everywhere by default) - PostgreSQL via Supabase (direct SQL for queries that benefit from it, ORM for CRUD) - Clerk for auth (team invitations already solved — don't rebuild it) - Tailwind CSS + shadcn/ui - TypeScript strict mode ## Core Domain - Project: top-level entity, owned by a GC organization - Milestone: a named phase with start/end dates, belongs to a project - Package: a bundle of deliverables within a milestone (has assigned parties) - Document: a file with versioning, attached to a package - DocumentRevision: a specific version of a document (current/superseded states) - Party: an organization invited to participate in a project or package - Approval: a review request tied to a specific document revision ## Approval State Machine Approvals move through exactly these states: DRAFT → SUBMITTED → UNDER_REVIEW → (APPROVED | REJECTED | SUPERSEDED) Transition rules (verified with domain expert): - DRAFT: only owner can advance to SUBMITTED - SUBMITTED: reviewer can move to UNDER_REVIEW (claiming it) or REJECTED - UNDER_REVIEW: reviewer can move to APPROVED or REJECTED - SUPERSEDED: occurs when a new document revision is uploaded while any approval is in SUBMITTED or UNDER_REVIEW state - APPROVED/REJECTED/SUPERSEDED: terminal states (no transitions out) ## What Must NOT Change - Clerk organization IDs (tied to existing subscriptions) - Database tables: projects, organizations (existing Supabase RLS policies) - Supabase Storage bucket names (files already stored there) ## Constraints - No third-party analytics (GDPR — enterprise customers require this) - All file storage via Supabase Storage (no S3 dependency) - Server components for data display — no useEffect data fetching - No background job infrastructure for v1 (keep it simple) ## Definition of Done A developer who has never seen this codebase opens src/app/ and knows what every route does. They don't need to read more than 2 additional files to fully understand any user operation end to end. ``` The document took about twenty-five minutes to write. Matt read it back twice, made some corrections to the approval state machine section (he'd initially gotten the superseded transition wrong — he had to check his notes from a customer conversation), and saved it. Then he sent his first actual message. "Read this CLAUDE.md carefully. I want to rebuild the core application described here. Before we write any code, I need: a proposed database schema, a proposed Next.js route structure, and an honest assessment of the three biggest technical risks in this project. I also need a recommended build order — what should we build first, second, and third, and why that order." The response took about four minutes. It was longer than Matt expected. The database schema proposal was clean and direct — fifteen tables, with explicit Postgres enum types for the approval states he'd described, foreign key constraints with sensible cascade semantics, and a detailed comment block above each table explaining its purpose. Not the purpose you could infer from the column names — the purpose in the domain: why this table exists, what question it answers, what would break if it didn't exist. The route structure was similarly clean. A directory tree showing the Next.js App Router layout, with route groups for authenticated vs. public views, a clear separation between GC-facing and sub-facing routes, and a note about which routes would be server-only vs. which needed client-side interactivity.
