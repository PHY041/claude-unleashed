# Chapter 2: Teaching Your AI Who You Are ## The Memory Garden *In which we discover that forty-three lines of text can change everything — and learn why the most important file on your computer is one you write yourself.* --- ## Opening: The Forty-Three Lines She wrote forty-three lines of text. They changed everything. Her name is Leila Nasser. She is a UX designer at a mid-size fintech company in Dubai. She had been using Claude Code for three months before she wrote those forty-three lines. In those three months, she had developed a mild, persistent frustration of the kind that is hard to name precisely. Something was not quite right. Claude was good — genuinely impressive, often brilliant — but every new session started from zero. Every morning she would explain her project again. Remind Claude of her preferences. Describe her stack. Correct the same categories of mistake she had corrected two weeks before. It felt like working with someone who had exceptional talent but no memory. As if you hired the best contractor in the city, had a long and productive first day, explained everything carefully, made progress — and then arrived the next morning to find they had no recollection of you. Not because they forgot, but because overnight they had become someone new. Same skill, same intelligence, same capability. No context. No history. No relationship. This is what Claude Code is without CLAUDE.md. Not a flaw — the model genuinely has no persistent memory between sessions. Each session is architecturally fresh, a clean slate. The intelligence arrives intact; the relationship does not. For three months, Leila Nasser paid the tax that this architecture imposes. She paid it in re-explanations. She paid it in corrections. She paid it in the invisible overhead of context-building that happened in every first exchange, before any real work could begin. Then a colleague showed her a file. --- ### The Week Before: A Specific Kind of Frustration To understand what those forty-three lines meant, you need to feel what life was like before them. Not in the abstract — in the particular texture of a real week. It was the week of November 17th, 2025. Leila was working on three different components across five sessions. The components were not related — a transaction filter, a notification banner, and a data export flow. Each session started fresh. Each session required the same invisible maintenance work. The transaction filter session, Monday morning: Leila opened Claude Code and described what she needed — a multi-select filter that would let users narrow transaction history by type, status, and date range. The first suggestion came back using `react-select`, a popular library. Leila stopped. They did not use `react-select`. The team had standardized on a custom component library they built themselves, specifically because `react-select` had accessibility issues they could not work around. She explained this. Claude adjusted. Second exchange: Claude proposed a state management approach that lifted the filter state up to the page component. Leila paused. They had decided three months ago to keep all filter state local to the component unless there was a specific reason to share it — a convention established after a messy refactor of a different page where over-lifted state had become impossible to follow. She explained this. Claude adjusted. Third exchange: Claude's code used hex color values directly in the styles. They had a design token system. Color names, not hex values, so that theme changes propagated automatically. She explained this. Claude adjusted. By the time the first real design decision of the session arrived — how to handle the state transition when all filters were cleared — Leila had already spent seventeen minutes doing context maintenance that should have been unnecessary. She knew this context. She had explained it a dozen times. There was simply no mechanism to have explained it once. Thursday, the notification banner session. Within eight minutes: styled-components suggestion (wrong — Tailwind), inline colors (wrong — design tokens), missing aria-label on the close button (wrong — WCAG 2.1 compliance was non-negotiable). Three corrections. Eight minutes. Context that should have been there from the first token. And Friday, the data export session. This one was the worst. She asked for help designing the file format selection UI — a fairly complex interaction involving radio buttons, format preview, and conditional options that appeared based on which format was selected. Claude's first draft was visually clean but used a complex nested conditional rendering pattern that was hard to follow. Leila commented that she preferred flat conditional patterns over nested ones. Claude adjusted. Then, in the same response, it introduced a `styled` component for the preview pane. Leila had just corrected this thirty minutes earlier in the same session. It had slipped. Not because Claude was bad — because context was thin, and thin context frays. This is the texture of working without CLAUDE.md. Not catastrophic. Not unusable. Just — expensive. In small doses, invisible. Across fifty sessions, the cost is a week of work. The cumulative effect of that week was not just the lost time. It was the quality degradation that comes from working under constant cognitive friction. When you are spending seventeen minutes establishing context before the real work begins, you arrive at the real work with less energy and less clarity than you would have had otherwise. The decisions you make in the fourth exchange of a session are shaped by the previous three exchanges. When three of those four exchanges were context maintenance rather than creative problem-solving, the decisions are worse. Leila did not measure this. She felt it. By Friday afternoon, she was tired in a way that was disproportionate to the amount of actual design thinking she had done. She had been productive in the superficial sense — code had been written, components had been built — but she felt like she had been running in sand. There is a compounding dynamic to this kind of friction that is easy to miss. The wasted time is visible — seventeen minutes on Monday morning, eight minutes on Thursday. But the secondary cost is less visible: the momentum you do not build. Creative and technical work has a texture to it. When a session starts with a genuine problem — something you have not solved, something that requires thought — you enter a state of focused engagement that compounds over the course of the session. The first real exchange is energized. The second builds on the first. The third follows from the second. By the end of a good session, you are doing your best thinking. When the first three exchanges are context maintenance, you lose the runway. You arrive at the first real exchange already a little depleted. The creative work happens, but it happens from a lower altitude. The solutions you reach are competent rather than excellent. The decisions you make at the end of a fatigued session are not your best decisions. This is not hypothetical. Anyone who has worked intensively with Claude Code for three months without a CLAUDE.md can feel this pattern. The sessions are productive. But there is always a sense that you are doing less than you could be. That the tool is capable of more, and so are you, if only the setup overhead could be eliminated. Leila felt exactly this on that November Friday afternoon. She did not have the diagnosis yet — she did not know what was causing the fatigue. She just knew something was wrong with the shape of her days. --- ### Tuesday Evening The following Tuesday, Leila's colleague Yasmin sat down next to her in the open office and pulled up a file on her laptop. "This changed everything for me," Yasmin said. "I made it in my second week. I keep wondering why no one told me about it." The file was Yasmin's CLAUDE.md. Forty-one lines of plain text. Nothing fancy. No special syntax. Just Yasmin, explaining who she was and what she needed. Leila read it. It was not technically impressive. It was not elaborate. It was a list of preferences, a description of a project, a note about the tech stack, two or three specific constraints. The kind of document you would write if someone asked you to brief a new contractor before their first day. But something clicked as she read it. She recognized it immediately — not the content, but the shape. This was the explanation she had been giving in the first exchange of every session for three months. This was the context maintenance. The tax she kept paying. All of it, written down once. She opened a new document and started writing. She wrote quickly, without overthinking — she had three months of frustrations stored up; the words came fast. She was not crafting a document. She was releasing a backlog. She wrote about Tailwind. She wrote about her accessibility standards. She wrote about the design token system. She wrote about her preference for flat conditional rendering, for immutable patterns, for plain-English explanations before code blocks. She wrote about the auth module that she should not touch without approval. She wrote about her project. Eleven minutes. Forty-three lines. She saved the file as `CLAUDE.md`, dropped it into the root of her project directory, and went home. On the drive home, she thought about what she had just done. It was an unusually specific kind of productive — the satisfaction of having organized a backlog that had been accumulating without her realizing it. The forty-three lines were not a new document. They were three months of corrections and re-explanations, finally written down in the right place. --- ### The Forty-Three Lines, Annotated Here is what she wrote, in full, with the meaning of each section: ```markdown # CLAUDE.md ## Who I Am UX designer with some coding ability. I work in TypeScript and React. I understand design systems, component architecture, and Figma deeply. I struggle with backend logic and need extra explanation there. My experience level: I can read code better than I can write it from scratch. ``` These four lines change every explanation Claude gives her. Not "I am a developer" — "I can read code better than I can write it from scratch." That phrase is worth a thousand corrections. It tells Claude: show me the pattern first. Explain what the code does before showing it. When I ask why, give me the why. Notice what is not in this section. She does not explain her company, her title, her team size, or her years of experience. She explains the one thing that matters for working with her: the specific asymmetry between her reading ability and her writing ability. That asymmetry determines how Claude should pitch explanations, how deep into theory it should go, and when it should ask for clarification rather than assuming. ```markdown ## This Project: Fintech Dashboard A React dashboard for financial advisors working with small business clients. "Not technical people" means Claude will not suggest exposing complex filters or raw data. "Clarity over cleverness" is a standing bias: when two approaches work, choose the one that reads more easily, not the one that is more elegant. The parenthetical — "not the advisors — the clients" — is the most important part. It distinguishes between two user populations with very different technical sophistication. Financial advisors are often technically sophisticated. Their clients — small business owners, sole proprietors — often are not.
