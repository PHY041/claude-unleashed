In the fall of 2025, Anthropic held its first major open hackathon — thirteen thousand applicants, five hundred selected, forty-eight hours to build something that mattered. The winners were not who anyone expected. Not the Stanford CS graduates who'd been writing code since middle school. Not the FAANG engineers with decades of industry experience and polished GitHub profiles. Not the startup founders with portfolios of shipped products and a fluency with frameworks that comes from years of daily use. The winners were a cardiologist, an attorney, and a road systems worker. People who had never shipped software professionally. People for whom "debugging" previously meant figuring out why a patient wasn't responding to medication, why a legal argument wasn't holding up in court, why a stretch of highway kept developing the same crack in the same place every spring. People who knew their domains with a precision that no junior developer could match, and who had spent careers watching software that was supposed to help them fall short in ways that only they could see. The judging panel for the hackathon had to make a decision they hadn't expected to face: how to rank projects built by people who couldn't explain the code they'd produced against projects built by professional engineers who could. The decision they made — to judge on impact, specificity of solution, and depth of understanding of the problem being solved — turned out to favor the domain experts. This is what the hackathon taught us. You can debate the judging criteria. You can argue about whether the cardiologist's monitoring application was architecturally sound, whether the attorney's permit review tool had robust error handling, whether the road worker's infrastructure prediction system had adequate test coverage. On all of these technical dimensions, the professional engineers probably built better. But on the dimension that the judging panel ultimately valued most — does this tool actually solve the problem it claims to solve, does it solve it in the way that the domain actually requires, does the builder understand the failure modes that matter — the domain experts won. Now, six months later, we find them again. The tools they built at the hackathon are out in the world. The world has changed, at least in small, specific, verifiable ways, because of what they made. But the more important thing has also changed: the people themselves have changed, not into software engineers, but into something that didn't have a name before. Something that might, with time, become the most important new professional category of the decade. --- **Dr. Marcus Webb** is making his rounds on the cardiac care floor at Cedars-Sinai Medical Center in Los Angeles when he stops to check his phone. Not for messages. Not for the news. For a dashboard. The dashboard is his own creation — a real-time monitoring application that pulls data from the ICU's cardiac monitors, processes it through a pattern-recognition layer he designed based on fifteen years of reading the electrical language of the human heart, and flags anomalies that might precede adverse events. The flagging logic is not generic. It does not flag what commercially available monitoring systems flag. It flags what Marcus knows to flag: the subtle relationships between drug interactions and QT interval changes, the threshold differences that matter for post-surgical patients versus medical admissions, the patterns that his hospital's specific patient population tends to show in the hours before a deterioration event. The application has been deployed in three hospitals. Not as a formal medical device — that path requires years and millions of dollars, a process designed for industrial-scale products, not for a physician's hypothesis tested in a forty-eight-hour sprint. Instead, it is deployed as an internal clinical decision support tool: a supplementary information source that physicians can use or ignore as their judgment dictates, approved through hospital informatics review processes that evaluate utility and risk in a more flexible way than the FDA's formal device clearance pathway. The distinction matters. Marcus navigated the approval process at Cedars-Sinai by doing something that a contracted developer building the same tool never could have done: he presented the evidence for the tool's value in clinical terms, to a committee of clinicians, drawing on his own peer-reviewed publications and his understanding of the outcomes literature. He knew which objections the ethics committee would raise before they raised them. He knew which data to present, which comparisons to make, which failure modes to document in language that would satisfy both the risk management team and the clinical chiefs. He is still a cardiologist. He used a cardiologist's credibility and a cardiologist's vocabulary to get a cardiologist's tool approved. No contractor, however skilled technically, could have done that for him. Marcus is forty-four years old. He did a residency in internal medicine and a fellowship in interventional cardiology. He has spent fifteen years studying the electrical language of the heart — learning it first in medical school as a series of waveforms and measurements, then in residency as a diagnostic tool, then in fellowship as a window into the mechanical and electrical pathophysiology of specific disease states, and finally in practice as a language he reads the way a musician reads a score, seeing not just what's on the page but what the performer is likely to do next. That knowledge is specific, deep, experiential in a way that cannot be quickly transferred, and impossible to fake. Before the hackathon, the software that ran the monitoring systems in his ICU was built by engineers who had never read an EKG in their lives. By product managers who thought in terms of user engagement rather than clinical utility. By companies that were very good at making software and very bad at understanding medicine — companies that had built systems capable of alarming on everything but providing signal about nothing, systems whose sensitivity was so high and whose specificity so low that the clinical response was to stop paying attention to the alarms entirely, because the background noise had made it impossible to distinguish signal from static. This is the alarm fatigue problem, and it is one of the most serious patient safety issues in hospital medicine. Hospitals have implemented it for themselves by deploying monitoring software that their clinical staff have stopped trusting, in service of regulatory compliance and liability management, without apparent awareness that a monitoring system clinicians ignore is worse than no monitoring system at all. Marcus knew this problem from the inside. He had been one of the clinicians who had learned to filter out the alarms. He had been one of the attendings in the room when a patient deteriorated because the alarm that should have alerted the team was buried under forty other alarms that meant nothing. The software he built at the hackathon — and has continued to refine in the six months since — is not more sensitive than the commercial alternatives. It is more specific. It alarms less. It is right more often. "I spent the first twelve hours completely frustrated," he told us, sitting in his office at the hospital, the dashboard running on the monitor behind him, showing the current status of every cardiac patient on the floor — green, green, green, one amber. "I kept thinking in the wrong direction. I kept trying to learn to code. I was looking at documentation for JavaScript charting libraries, trying to understand what 'async' meant, wondering if I needed to learn about servers. And then something clicked." He stopped. He looked at his hands for a moment. "And then I stopped trying to learn to code and I started just... describing the problem. The exact problem. In the language I actually know." The shift he describes is real and it is not trivial. There is a cognitive transition required to use Claude Code effectively that many people — especially technically literate people who have been taught that computers require you to speak their language — find difficult to make. The transition is from implementation thinking to specification thinking. From "how do I make this" to "what do I need, precisely, and why." Marcus, because he had no pre-existing habits about how computers worked, because he had no intermediate layer of programming knowledge that his mind naturally reached for, found the transition easier once he understood what he was actually being asked to do. The turning point came when he stopped writing prompts like *"make a graph that shows heart rate over time"* and started writing prompts like *"I need to visualize the relationship between QTc interval prolongation and the simultaneous administration of QT-prolonging medications, with automatic flagging when the combination risk score exceeds the Tisdale risk threshold of 7. The visualization should show the trend over the preceding 6 hours, not just the current value, because clinically what matters is whether the QTc is stable, trending up slowly, or accelerating. The Tisdale score needs to account for which specific drugs are on board — a patient on amiodarone plus a macrolide antibiotic is in a different risk category than a patient on a low-dose antipsychotic alone."* He knew what all of that meant. He knew why it mattered. He knew exactly which clinical decision it was supposed to support, and what decisions it was explicitly not supposed to replace. Claude Code had never heard of the Tisdale risk score before that conversation. But it could look it up. It could understand the mathematical relationship. It could understand that "trend" in this context means something specific and clinical. It could write the code that implemented all of it. What it could not have done — what no one who wasn't a cardiologist could have done — was know that this was the right thing to build in the first place, or why the trending rather than the instantaneous value was what the clinician actually needed. "That's the thing everyone misses," Marcus says, standing up, walking to the monitor, pointing at the amber indicator. "The AI doesn't know what matters. I know what matters. The AI knows how to build things. That's a partnership that makes both sides more powerful." He is watching the amber indicator. It is trending. He makes a note. His application is now deployed in three hospitals, with two more in active discussions. The second hospital — a community facility in the San Fernando Valley — adopted it six weeks after Cedars-Sinai, after a cardiologist who had heard about it asked Marcus to present it at grand rounds. The third, a cardiac specialty center in San Diego, sent an unsolicited email asking if they could implement it. The institutional pathway for a physician-built clinical decision support tool turns out to be navigable when the physician has the right credentials and the clinical evidence. The standard IT procurement process — which can take years for a commercial vendor relationship — moves faster when the tool's developer is also on the medical staff and can personally vouch for it to the chief medical officer. He has been offered money for the software. Not a small amount of money — health tech valuations are generous in 2026, and anything that credibly addresses alarm fatigue is worth paying for. He has declined, so far, for reasons that are partly practical and partly philosophical.
