# Chapter 1: What Is Claude Code, Really? *In which we discover that the most powerful AI tool in history looks like nothing at all — just a blinking cursor on a dark screen.* --- ## Section 1: Sarah's Tuesday ### The Kitchen Table at 9:47 PM Sarah Chen teaches seventh-grade science at a public school in Portland, Oregon. She has been teaching for eleven years. She has 142 students across five periods. She has a grading backlog that haunts her weekends. She has a classroom sink that has been dripping since October, a requisition form for its repair that she filed in November, and a maintenance team that has, so far, responded with the particular silence that public-school teachers learn to interpret as "eventually." Her husband Marcus coaches middle-school soccer. He wakes up at five in the morning and falls asleep on the couch by nine-thirty, reliably, in the middle of whatever they were watching together. He means well. He is exhausted. The kitchen table, on this particular Tuesday in November, is in a state that would be alarming to anyone who did not know Sarah. Papers, yes — but specific papers, organized in a way that makes sense only to her, in stacks that represent different states of completion. Permission slips for the December field trip to the science museum, sorted by period. A pile of parent emails she has read but not yet replied to. And the new stack, the one she has been avoiding since after dinner: 142 student lab reports. Lab reports submitted in formats that have multiplied beyond any reasonable expectation. There are PDFs — most of the PDFs are formatted the way she asked, student name at the top, typed. Good. There are Google Docs links, which require her to open each one in a browser, some of which are shared with commenting access and some of which, for reasons she will never fully understand, are shared view-only and cannot even be copied from. There is a Microsoft Word document from Ethan Kowalski, whose family moved from Ohio in October and whose laptop still runs Office and has never connected to Google Drive and probably never will. And there are three photographs. Three actual photographs, taken on phones at angles that suggest haste and poor lighting, of handwritten lab reports on notebook paper. These belong to Clio Ramirez, Destiny Washington, and a student she knows only by his preferred nickname, T-Bone, who is actually Thomas Bonneville but who has been T-Bone since third grade and will almost certainly go through high school that way. They do not have reliable computer access at home. They did their science homework the way students did homework in 1987, on paper with a pen, and they submitted it the way 2026 allows them to submit it: by photograph. Sarah is not resentful of these three. She is, if anything, more invested in their success than in students with better resources, because the research on this is consistent and she has seen it in eleven years of teaching: the students who have to work hardest to turn in what they turn in are often the most earnest about it. T-Bone's handwritten lab report, she already knows, will be methodical and precise. He takes science seriously. He has seen too many things go wrong in the world, she suspects, to take anything casually. So: 142 lab reports. PDFs, Google Docs, one Word file, three photographs. And it is 9:47 PM on a Tuesday. Her system, which she has refined across eight years of teaching this course: open each file, read it, copy the student name into her spreadsheet, check off each of the five required sections — hypothesis, materials, procedure, results, conclusion — note the missing ones, assign a preliminary grade based on the rubric she developed in her third year and has updated twice since then, add a comment specific enough to be useful in the gradebook. Do this 142 times. Four hours. Usually four hours, if she starts before ten and keeps moving. Tonight she is starting at 9:47. She will be done by two. She will sleep until six-fifteen and be in her classroom at seven-forty-five. She has done this so many times that it has stopped feeling like a problem. It is just what teaching is, in her mind. You teach, you grade. It takes as long as it takes. Her mother was a teacher. Her grandmother was a teacher. Grading was always something that happened late at night, at the kitchen table, while the rest of the family slept. Tonight, she does something different. --- ### The Weight of the Grading Night Before we get to Justine's text, it is worth spending a moment with what grading nights actually cost. Sarah has been doing them for eleven years. Not every night — but every grading cycle, which comes six or eight times per year depending on the assignment type. Lab reports are the longest. Eleven years of lab report grading nights: roughly fifty, at four hours each, is two hundred hours. That is five forty-hour work weeks, just for this one type of assignment. She does not think of it this way. She has never added it up. It is just what the job requires, distributed across years in small enough units that the total is invisible. Each individual night feels like "tonight's work," not "a fraction of the annual cost of doing this job." This is how inefficiency hides. Not in dramatic examples of wasted time, but in the quiet accumulation of small costs that never get totaled because they are spread across enough time that no single instance feels worth questioning. The four hours is just Tuesday night. The system of Tuesdays is not visible as a system. When you see the total — when you do the arithmetic, or when a technology makes the total visible by collapsing it into forty minutes — the emotional response is often disproportionate to the immediate saving. It is not that you saved four hours tonight. It is that you can see, for the first time, the shape of the cost you have been paying for years. And the shape is larger than you thought. This is why Sarah cried. Not about tonight. About the years. --- ### The Text That Started Everything Three weeks earlier, Justine Huang — Sarah's college roommate, now a project manager at a software company in Seattle — had sent a text. The text included a screenshot of a terminal window and a caption: "you need to try this." Sarah had filed this in the category of well-meaning suggestions from people who work in tech. She had, over the years, received many such suggestions. She had tried a document organization app that required three weeks of setup before it would do anything and then organized her documents in a way that made no sense. She had tried an AI essay-detection tool that flagged three of her best students as likely cheaters and gave no feedback on the actual essays. She had tried the grading feature in her school's learning management system, which worked beautifully for multiple-choice tests and had essentially no functionality for open-ended work. "You don't have to know anything about coding," Justine had said in a follow-up text. "You just talk to it." "I don't have time to learn a new tool," Sarah had replied. "That's the point," Justine had written back. "There's nothing to learn. You describe what you want. It does it. That's it." Sarah had been politely skeptical in the way that people who have been disappointed by technology promises become politely skeptical: not dismissive, because she does not dismiss things she has not tried, but not hopeful either. She had asked Justine to send her a link, downloaded the thing during a planning period the following Monday, and left it sitting in her Applications folder for the rest of that week. Tonight, finally, she opens it. --- ### The First Question The interface is exactly what Justine showed her in the screenshot. A dark window. A blinking cursor. No buttons. No icons. No instructions on the screen beyond a brief welcome message and a prompt. Sarah stares at it for a moment, feeling the particular self-consciousness of someone doing a new thing when there is nobody watching but the sensation of being watched anyway. She types the way she would type anything: carefully, describing what she actually has and what she actually needs. ``` I have 142 student lab reports in different formats in my ~/Downloads/lab-reports/ folder. I need to: 1. Read each one 2. Check if they have all 5 required sections (hypothesis, materials, procedure, results, conclusion) 3. Grade them on a rubric I'll describe 4. Output a spreadsheet with student name, missing sections, grade, and my notes Can you help me? ``` There is a pause. Then Claude responds. Not with a disclaimer. Not with a list of features she could unlock if she upgraded to a premium tier. Not with a suggestion to contact customer support. With a question. ``` I'd be glad to help. Before I start reading the files, let me ask a few things: 1. What's your grading rubric? You mentioned you'd describe it — please share it whenever you're ready. Even a rough description is fine; I can ask clarifying questions as I go. 2. For the "notes" column — do you want these to be specific (like "Missing procedure section; results section lacks quantitative data") or more general feedback phrases you'd paste into your gradebook? Knowing that helps me calibrate how detailed to be. 3. I can see the folder contains PDFs and what look like image files. The PDFs I can read directly. For image files of handwritten work, I can use OCR — optical character recognition — to extract the text. The accuracy won't be perfect for handwriting, but it's usually good enough for checking structure and content. Should I proceed with that approach for the image files? 4. Are there any students whose work should be treated differently — for instance, students with accommodations that affect what sections they're required to include? ``` Sarah reads this response twice. The fourth question stops her. She has a student with a 504 plan — an accommodation document — who has permission to skip the written hypothesis and submit a diagram instead. She had not thought to mention this. Claude Code had not known about it. But the shape of the question tells her something: this system is thinking about edge cases. It is not just processing her request. It is reasoning about what a grading task actually involves. She types back: her rubric (she pastes in the Word document she wrote in 2019), the specification that notes should be specific, confirmation that OCR is fine for the photos, and the note about the 504 student. Claude says: *Got it. Starting now.* --- ### The Forty-Three Minutes What follows is forty-three minutes of watching something work. Claude does not process the reports silently.
